      <h2>Description</h2>

      <p>
      What does it mean to live through, remember, and bear witness to violence and displacement across generations? This collection explores this question in the context of Palestine, building on sources that examine the impacts of public and mental health, policy and international law, and media representation. It brings together sources that recognize both the historical and social events that shaped modern Palestine, as well as the impact of the current (2023–2026; ongoing at the time of writing) conflict on Palestinian people, including physical violence, social disruption, mental health consequences, and media portrayal. The materials include text-based historical, statistical, experimental, and philosophical sources, alongside visual imagery sources that depict historical and modern war and displacement.
      </p>

      <p>
      A central theme in the collection is mental health, with a particular focus on the impacts of war on the mental health and daily life of children and adolescents. These survey-based analytical articles measure and describe how war shapes the daily lives and psychological well-being of children. While trends suggest high rates of mental disorder and the premature loss of youthful innocence, the same studies also show a strong resilience within the Palestinian population, including coping mechanisms that strengthen their communities.
      </p>

      <p>
      The second key theme traces historical events of violence and displacement, including the 1936 Arab Revolt, the 1948 Nakba, the 1948 War, and the Lebanese Civil War. These sources show that these events are remembered and interpreted in many different ways, and explore how personal and collective memory is shaped by emotion, trauma, and public narrative rather than fixed in an objective record. They also suggest a cyclical relationship between violence and displacement, driven by both external forces (such as occupation and segregation) and local responses. Continuous segregation and displacement can intensify violence by removing moderating influences and increasing competition for leadership, further reproducing instability.
      </p>

      <p>
      The third key theme examines how violent historical events are represented in journalism, literature, and official accounts, and how these narratives shape public perception. Photography collections and newspapers with images depict the severity of violence and displacement experienced by Palestinians, while analyses of textbooks, reports, novels, and testimonies show how narratives both reflect and influence understanding.
      </p>

      <p>
      The final theme centers on policy and international law, documenting both the historical policies that contributed to the conflict and the responses of the international community to the ongoing (2023–2026) crisis in Gaza. These sources highlight the role of policy decisions, as well as global perception and action (or inaction), and point to the limitations of current international legal frameworks in addressing the conflict and ensuring accountability.
      </p>
